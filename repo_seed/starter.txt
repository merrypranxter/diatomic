## Big-picture concept
Diatoms have silica cell walls (frustules) made of two overlapping valves (“lid” and “base”) connected by a silica girdle; their frustules come in two main morphological families: radially symmetric centric forms and bilaterally symmetric pennate forms, each with huge variation in outline and surface patterns. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frustule)
Those shapes can be described and classified with geometry (radial vs bilateral symmetry, aspect ratios, curvature) and with more formal methods like parametric curves and elliptic Fourier descriptors applied to valve outlines. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6487182/)

Your repo can be the bridge between “microscope art” and “math/GLSL toys”:

- A visual field guide to diatom forms (centric/pennate + subtypes).  
- Mathematical descriptors and generative scripts that reproduce those forms.  
- Curated references and image sources for anyone who wants to go deeper.

## Math and generative models section
This is where you nerd out on “how to parametrize a diatom”.

In `docs/03_math_models.md` and `/notebooks/`, you can progressively escalate from soft to hardcore:

1. **Geometric primitives & symmetry**

   - Parameterize simple centric forms as circles or ellipses with radial ribs and pores (think polar coordinates, \( r(\theta) = R + \epsilon(\theta) \) where \(\epsilon\) is a periodic modulation). [bioengineering.hyperbook.mcgill](https://bioengineering.hyperbook.mcgill.ca/mathematical-models-of-diatoms-understanding-their-complex-shape-reproduction-and-chain-formation/)
   - Parameterize pennate forms as curves in 2D whose outline is defined by a smooth centerline and a width function, then mirror across the midline for bilateral symmetry. [mathematik.uni-kassel](https://www.mathematik.uni-kassel.de/~seiler/Papers/PDF/DiatomReview.pdf)

2. **Elliptic Fourier Descriptors (EFDs)**

   - EFDs are widely used to encode diatom valve outlines for identification: take the closed contour, decompose into Fourier coefficients, and reconstruct the shape with a small number of harmonics. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6487182/)
   - You can add a notebook like `notebooks/efd_diatom_outlines.ipynb` that:  
     - Loads an outline from `/data/outlines/`,  
     - Computes EFDs,  
     - Reconstructs the contour with 5, 10, 20 harmonics and overlays plots.  

3. **Fractals, golden ratios, L-systems**

   - Some authors explicitly frame diatom geometry using fractal dimensions, golden ratios, and L-systems to explain repeating patterns and branching morphologies on frustules. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1874778717301265)
   - You could do a “myth vs math” treatment: show where golden ratios actually show up in real measurements, vs where they’re just romantic metaphors, and how fractal dimension can be estimated from pore distribution patterns. [bioengineering.hyperbook.mcgill](https://bioengineering.hyperbook.mcgill.ca/mathematical-models-of-diatoms-understanding-their-complex-shape-reproduction-and-chain-formation/)

4. **Physics-ish models (optional deep dive)**

   - Connect to literature that treats frustules as photonic crystals or hierarchical mechanical structures; you don’t have to reproduce the physics, but you can point to them as inspiration for shader tricks (diffraction, scattering, band-pass textures). [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8282634/)
   - For each theme, sketch out what a “toy model” in GLSL would look like (e.g., turn a porous lattice into an interference pattern, or model light attenuation through a perforated slab).

```markdown
A visual and mathematical atlas of diatoms — tiny silica architectures with outrageous symmetry, pattern, and micro-geometry.

## What is a diatom?

Diatoms are unicellular microalgae whose cell wall is a glassy silica shell called a **frustule**.[web:20][web:28]  
The frustule is built from two overlapping halves (thecae or valves) that fit together like a petri dish, with the joint reinforced by silica girdle bands.[web:20][web:23][web:49]  

In **valve view** you see the face of a valve (disc, ellipse, boat, etc.); in **girdle view** you see the side, where the two valves and their girdle bands read as a box or cylinder.[web:23][web:50]

At the gross level, diatoms fall into two big shape families:

- **Centric diatoms** – radially symmetric, often circular or polygonal in valve view.[web:23][web:28]  
- **Pennate diatoms** – bilaterally symmetric, often elongated or boat‑shaped in valve view.[web:23][web:28]

Within those two families is a wild zoo of outlines, pore lattices, ribs, spines, and slits that can all be treated as geometry.
```

```markdown
# Overview

This project treats diatoms as a space where **biology**, **geometry**, and **image‑making** collide.

Diatoms are single‑celled microalgae with silica cell walls called **frustules** that carry species‑specific architectures of pores, ribs, and spines.[web:20][web:26]  
They contribute an estimated 20–25% of global primary production, but here we are mostly obsessed with their shapes and the math behind them.[web:26][web:54]  

From a distance, many diatoms read as simple discs or boats; under magnification they reveal complex, hierarchical patterns that behave like mechanical scaffolds and photonic lattices.[web:14][web:16][web:54]  

---

## Goals

This repo aims to be:

- A **visual field guide** to diatom forms – centric and pennate, plus sub‑families of outlines and ornamentation.[web:23][web:28]  
- A **geometry notebook** – collecting ways to parameterize these shapes: symmetry, curves, elliptic Fourier descriptors, and more speculative models.[web:21][web:24][web:33]  
- A **sandbox for code and shaders** – tools to extract outlines from images, measure shape features, and generate diatom‑like structures in GLSL or other media.[web:21][web:33]

- **Shape families** – centric vs pennate; within each, recurring outlines (discs, polygons, rods, naviculoid boats, cymbelloid bananas, sigmoids, ribbons).[web:23][web:31][web:49]  
- **Pattern scales** – coarse outline, mid‑scale rib/stria patterns, fine‑scale nanopore lattices.[web:14][web:26]  

Each of these can be drawn, parameterized, and then abused in code.
```

## docs/01_structure_frustule.md

```markdown
# Frustule structure

The diatom cell wall is a silica shell called a **frustule**, built from two overlapping halves plus a set of silica bands that link them together.[web:20][web:23][web:51]  
Understanding this basic architecture makes it much easier to read microscope images and to design generative models of diatom shapes.

---

## Thecae (valves) and girdle

The frustule is divided into two main parts:

- **Valves (thecae)** – two rigid, plate‑like halves of the shell that overlap slightly.[web:20][web:28][web:51]  
- **Girdle (cingulum)** – the banded region between the valves, made of multiple silica **girdle bands** (copulae) that hold the valves together like hoops around a barrel.[web:20][web:50][web:51]  

The two valves are slightly different in size:

- The **epitheca** is the slightly larger “lid” valve.  
- The **hypotheca** is the slightly smaller “base” valve that fits under the epitheca.[web:20][web:28][web:51]

During cell division, each daughter cell keeps one old valve and builds one new one, which is why one lineage tends to get progressively smaller until it resets via an auxospore stage.[web:20][web:26]

---

## Valve view vs girdle view

Because the frustule is a 3D box or cylinder, diatoms look very different depending on orientation:

- **Valve view** – looking straight at a valve face; you see the outline (disc, ellipse, boat, etc.) and the radial or axial arrangement of striae and pores.[web:23][web:49]  
- **Girdle view** – looking at the side of the frustule; you see a box or banded cylinder with the two valves at the ends and the girdle bands in between.[web:23][web:50][web:51]  

Many keys and morphological descriptions specify which view they are talking about, so this repo does the same; generative models may explicitly target one view at a time.

---

## Surface features

Valve and girdle surfaces are perforated and sculpted in species‑specific ways:

- **Areolae** – individual pores that pass through the silica; often arranged into rows.[web:20][web:52]  
- **Striae** – lines or rows of areolae, usually visible in light microscopy as fine lines.[web:23][web:49]  
- **Costae and ribs** – thicker silica bars between rows of pores, reading as radiating spokes or axial ribs.[web:49][web:52]  
- **Spines and marginal processes** – protrusions that help link cells into chains or influence fluid interactions.[web:14][web:26]  

At nanoscales, areolae and related openings can form ordered lattices with pore diameters on the order of a few hundred nanometres, which strongly affect optical and mechanical behaviour.[web:14][web:20][web:26]

---

## Raphe and motility (pennate special)

Many pennate diatoms have a **raphe**, a slit‑like structure that runs along the valve and secretes mucilage for gliding motility.[web:23][web:50][web:52]  

Key points:

- Some genera have raphes on both valves (e.g., *Navicula*), some on one, some on neither.[web:23]  
- The raphe can be central, marginal, or traverse onto the valve mantle; canal raphes sit within a channel supported by silica bridges (fibulae).[web:50][web:52]  
- Raphe architecture (length, curvature, interruptions, position) is a crucial taxonomic feature and also a beautiful curve motif for generative models.[web:23][web:31][web:50]

In shape terms, the raphe is often an axial curve that suggests a natural coordinate system for parameterizing the rest of the valve.

---

## Centric vs pennate architecture

The same structural vocabulary plays out differently in centric and pennate diatoms:

- **Centric diatoms**
  - Valve outline typically circular, polygonal, or cylindrical in valve view.[web:23][web:28]  
  - Striae and ribs are arranged radially or in concentric rings around a centre or annulus.[web:23][web:25]  
  - Often lack a raphe; movement is typically passive or chain‑based.[web:23][web:28]

- **Pennate diatoms**
  - Valve outline typically elongated and bilaterally symmetric in valve view (naviculoid boats, cymbelloid bananas, sigmoids, etc.).[web:23][web:28][web:31]  
  - Ornamentation organized along an apical axis, with or without a raphe system; axial and central areas are key landmarks.[web:31][web:52]  

This repo leans into that architecture when grouping shapes in the catalog and when designing parametric models.

---

## Why structure matters for math and art

The frustule’s hierarchical structure — outline, ribs, pores, nano‑lattices — is where geometry and function meet:

- Biologically, these structures relate to mechanical protection, buoyancy, filtering, and sophisticated light manipulation.[web:14][web:16][web:18][web:54]  
- Geometrically, they provide nested symmetry operations, repeat units, and feature scales that can be captured in curves, tilings, and texture synthesis.[web:24][web:26][web:54]  

When building generative diatom forms in code or shaders, this doc is the checklist of which structural elements you want to honour, exaggerate, or break.
```

```markdown
# Frustule structure

The diatom cell wall is a silica shell called a **frustule**, built from two overlapping valves and a banded girdle region.[web:20][web:23][web:51]  
Understanding this architecture is the basis for how shapes are grouped and modeled in this repo.

---

## Valves and girdle

- **Valves (thecae)** – two rigid plates that overlap like a petri dish:
  - **Epitheca** – slightly larger "lid".
  - **Hypotheca** – slightly smaller "base".[web:20][web:28][web:51]
- **Girdle (cingulum)** – series of silica **girdle bands** that encircle the cell and connect the valves.[web:20][web:50][web:51]

During cell division, each daughter cell inherits one old valve and builds one new one, leading to progressive size reduction until a size‑resetting auxospore stage.[web:20][web:26]

---

## Valve view vs girdle view

Diatoms look very different depending on orientation:

- **Valve view** – looking straight at a valve face; shows the outline (disc, ellipse, boat, etc.) and arrangement of striae and pores.[web:23][web:49]
- **Girdle view** – looking at the side; shows the depth of the frustule and stacked girdle bands.[web:23][web:50]

Catalog entries explicitly state which view images and outlines correspond to.

---

## Surface features

Common features referenced in the catalog:

- **Areolae** – pores perforating the silica.[web:20][web:52]
- **Striae** – rows of areolae; often the main visible texture in light microscopy.[web:23][web:49]
- **Ribs / costae** – thicker silica bars between pores.[web:49][web:52]
- **Spines & processes** – protrusions that can link cells into chains or affect hydrodynamics.[web:14][web:26]

Nanometre‑scale pore lattices and sub‑structures give frustules unusual optical and mechanical properties and are an inspiration for some of the math and shaders.[web:14][web:16][web:54]
```

```markdown
# Morphology and shape families

Classical diatom morphology divides forms into **centric** and **pennate** based largely on symmetry and outline.[web:23][web:28][web:49]  
This repo overlays a more visual “shape family” taxonomy on top of that to organize the catalog.

---

## Centric vs pennate

- **Centric diatoms**
  - Radial or rotational symmetry in valve view.
  - Outlines: discs, polygons, sometimes elongate cylinders in girdle view.
  - Ornamentation arranged radially or in concentric patterns.[web:23][web:25]

- **Pennate diatoms**
  - Bilateral symmetry along a long axis in valve view (sometimes with asymmetry about a second axis).
  - Outlines: naviculoid boats, cymbelloid bananas, gomphonemoid clubs, sigmoids, ribbons.[web:23][web:31][web:49]
  - Ornamentation organized along an apical axis, often with a raphe system.[web:31][web:52]

---

## Shape families used here

Examples of shape families used for catalog grouping:

- **Centric / disc radiate** – near‑circular valves with radial striae.
- **Centric / polygonal & stellate** – triangular, hexagonal, starburst outlines.
- **Centric / cylindrical chain formers** – cylinders forming chains in girdle view.

- **Pennate / naviculoid boats** – elongated, pointed ends, central raphe.
- **Pennate / cymbelloid bananas** – curved “banana” outlines with dorsal/ventral asymmetry.
- **Pennate / sigmoid and wavy** – S‑shaped or gently sinuous axes.
- **Pennate / ribbon‑forming** – long chains or bands in girdle view.

Each catalog page under `catalog/centric` or `catalog/pennate` states which family it belongs to, the view (valve/girdle), and the key visual decisions behind that grouping.
```

```markdown
# Math and geometry models

This document sketches the mathematical tools used to describe and generate diatom forms.

---

## Outlines as curves

Valve outlines can be treated as closed planar curves:

- Simple shapes (circles, ellipses) model centric discs and elongated rods.
- More complex curves model naviculoid, cymbelloid, and sigmoid outlines:
  - A centerline curve plus a width function mirrored across a midline is a natural way to represent many pennate forms.[web:24][web:30]

---

## Symmetry

Key symmetry types:

- **Radial / rotational** – centric discs and stars.
- **Bilateral** – pennate forms with a single mirror axis.
- **Translational / chain** – ribbon‑forming or chain‑forming taxa in girdle view.[web:24][web:47]

These symmetries guide both outline parameterizations and pattern tilings in shaders.

---

## Elliptic Fourier descriptors (EFDs)

Elliptic Fourier descriptors are used in the literature to encode diatom valve outlines and compare shapes quantitatively.[web:21][web:33]  

Here they serve to:

- Compress outlines from `data/outlines/` into a small set of coefficients.
- Reconstruct outlines at different harmonic counts in notebooks.
- Map multiple shapes into a low‑dimensional “shape space”.

See `notebooks/02_elliptic_fourier_descriptors.ipynb` for a worked example.

---

## Hierarchy and pattern

Frustules have nested structure (outline → ribs → pores → nanopores), and several reviews discuss links between morphology, mechanics, and optics.[web:14][web:26][web:54]  

In this repo, we treat this hierarchy as:

- A design pattern for multi‑layer shaders and textures.
- A prompt for toy models of light scattering or mechanical stiffness, not full physical simulations.

Details live in the notebooks and shader comments rather than here.
```

```markdown
## Visual description

Elongated valves with:

Common features:
```

```glsl
// Pennate naviculoid boat – placeholder
// Corresponds to catalog/pennate/naviculoid_boat_shapes.md

#ifdef GL_ES
precision mediump float;
#endif

uniform vec2 iResolution;
uniform float iTime;

float boatShape(vec2 p) {
    // TODO: replace with a nicer centerline + width function.
    p.x = abs(p.x);
    float halfWidth = mix(0.1, 0.02, p.x); // tapered ends
    return smoothstep(halfWidth, halfWidth - 0.01, abs(p.y));
}

void main() {
    vec2 uv = (gl_FragCoord.xy / iResolution.xy) * 2.0 - 1.0;
    uv.x *= iResolution.x / iResolution.y;

    float body = boatShape(uv);
    gl_FragColor = vec4(vec3(body), 1.0);
}
```
